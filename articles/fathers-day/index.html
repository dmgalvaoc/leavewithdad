Life · Father's Day 2026

Why I Built This

Today is Father's Day. So before I write another word about permits, or HOA rules, or the best beer fridge for a South Florida garage — I want to tell you why this website exists at all. Because the name isn't random. And the answer is more personal than you might think.

D
Dad
June 2026 · Plantation, FL

I grew up in Brazil. I moved to South Florida as an adult — with a wife, ambitions, and the very reasonable assumption that I would figure things out as I went. And I did figure them out. Eventually. After wasting time I didn't have, spending money I didn't need to spend, and asking questions I would have been embarrassed to ask out loud.

Nobody had told me about alarm permits. Nobody mentioned that the HOA could hold up a closing. Nobody warned me that what you plant in August in South Florida is completely different from what you'd plant anywhere else on the planet. I learned all of it the hard way — through trial, error, phone calls that went nowhere, and PDF documents with broken links on city websites that hadn't been updated since 2014.

There is a specific kind of knowledge that doesn't live on the internet. It lives with people who've already been through it. The contractor who did your neighbor's roof. The guy at the hardware store who's been selling concrete mix in this county for twenty years. Your father-in-law, if you're lucky enough to have one who knows things. That guy.

I didn't always have that guy. So I became him — slowly, one project at a time.

The Name

"Leave With Dad" started as a joke between me and my wife. Every time something broke, or we needed something figured out, or there was a decision to make that required more research than either of us had time for — the answer was always the same: leave it with Dad.

Meaning: I'll look into it. I'll call the right people. I'll read the actual statute. I'll find out what the permit actually requires, and whether we actually need one, and what happens if we don't get one. Give it to me and I'll come back with an answer.

That's not a brag. That's just what dads do. Or at least, what this dad does. Because the alternative — not knowing, not figuring it out, leaving it unresolved — is not something I'm comfortable with. I am the engineer who buys a $21 refrigerant detector on Amazon at 10pm on a Friday before a holiday weekend because two technicians couldn't find my leak and I refuse to accept "we don't know" as an answer.

My wife says I have a problem. She's not wrong. But the house is cool and the beer fridge is full, so I'll take it.

What This Place Actually Is

Leave With Dad is not a listicle site. It's not a sponsored content farm pretending to be a neighborhood blog. It's not trying to rank for every keyword Google has ever indexed about Broward County.

It's the stuff I've actually done, in the actual house I actually live in, in the actual city I actually moved to — Plantation, Florida — and everything I wish someone had handed me when I got here. The alarm permit I didn't know I needed. The HOA rule I didn't know existed until I was already on the board helping enforce it. The concrete slab I poured myself for the first time on a Saturday with five bags of gravel and a borrowed tamper and absolutely no idea if I was doing it right. (I was. Mostly.)

It's also the stuff that makes this life worth living, not just survivable. The espresso maker my wife forced me to upgrade to, that I secretly love more than the manual one I had for twenty years. The beer fridge in the garage that keeps the first sip exactly as cold as it should be. The golf courses in Plantation where, if you're lucky, you can squeeze in a few hours on a weekend between the school runs and the home projects and the calls that go too long.

It's all of it. The practical and the personal. The permit and the pint. The HOA and the happy hour. Because that's what a dad's life actually looks like — it's not all hardware store runs and regulation PDFs. There are good parts, too. And they deserve a place here as well.

That's what this new tab — Life — is for.

Who This Is For

It's for the dad who just moved to Plantation and doesn't know where to start.

It's for the mom who has somehow ended up handling the permit application, the HOA dispute, and the HVAC warranty claim simultaneously while also having a job and a kid who needs to be somewhere at 3pm.

It's for the person who Googled "what grass do I have in my South Florida yard" and got seventeen conflicting answers from forums that haven't been updated since Obama's first term.

It's for anyone who ever said — out loud or quietly, to themselves, in the car after a long day — I really wish someone had just told me that.

Because somebody should have. And now someone will.

On Being a Dad

My kid is seven. On most mornings, I'm the one who does the school drop-off. We have a system: the espresso machine runs while I pack the bag, I drink it in about four minutes, and we're in the car by 7:45. On the days my wife goes to the office, I'm also doing afternoon pickup. On the days I have too many calls, she takes it. We split it, we adjust, we figure it out.

What nobody tells you about being a working parent is that it's not hard in a dramatic way. It's hard in a logistical way. The coordination required to keep a household running — the school calendar and the work calendar and the HOA meeting and the dentist appointment and the thing that's due and the thing that needs to be replaced and the thing that you said you'd look into — is a project management problem that never has a completion date.

You just manage it. Continuously. Indefinitely. And every once in a while, you carve out a Saturday afternoon for a tee time, or a Sunday evening for a cold beer, and you remember that the reason you're managing all of it is because the life underneath it is a good one.

I have a good life. I know that. I've lived in multiple countries. I've had harder days and easier days. I've made choices that paid off and choices I'd do differently. But I am here — in Plantation, Florida, with a seven-year-old who thinks I know everything, and a wife who knows I don't but tolerates it graciously, and a house that I am perpetually maintaining, upgrading, arguing with, and proud of.

That's the life. And this is the website that comes out of it.

A Note for Other Dads

If you're reading this on Father's Day — happy Father's Day. I hope you got the morning off. I hope someone made you coffee, or at least didn't make you make the coffee. I hope there's something cold waiting for you later.

And if you're in the middle of a project right now, or dealing with something the city website isn't explaining clearly, or trying to figure out what to do about the HOA letter you got last week — take a breath. You'll figure it out. You always do.

That's what we do.

That's what dads do.

Leave it with Dad. I'll look into it.

D
