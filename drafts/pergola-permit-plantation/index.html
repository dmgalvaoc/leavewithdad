Pergola Permit Nightmare — What I Learned Getting a Pergola Permitted in Plantation, FL

I wanted to add a pergola to my backyard. Shade, some structure, a place to actually sit outside without melting. Seemed simple. It was not. Here’s everything I wish I’d known before I started — including the Hurricane Zone rule that caught me completely off guard.

If you’re planning to build a pergola in Plantation — whether it’s a freestanding structure in the middle of your yard or something attached to the back of your house — you’re going to need a pergola permit. That’s the short answer. The longer answer involves engineer drawings, Hurricane Zone certifications, two separate approval tracks (the City and your HOA), and a processing timeline that can stretch longer than you’d expect.

I went through this. Not painlessly. This article is the guide I wish existed before I started.

Yes, You Need a Permit — Almost Every Time

Florida doesn’t let you build outdoor structures on the honor system. The City of Plantation requires a building permit before you can build, enlarge, or alter any structure, including backyard pergolas. This applies to both attached and freestanding designs.

The one grey area: freestanding structures under 120 square feet sometimes fall outside the permit threshold depending on jurisdiction, and some counties in Florida allow small accessory structures without a full permit. Plantation sits within Broward County’s jurisdiction and applies Broward’s standards — which are strict. My strong advice is to call Building Safety before assuming you’re exempt. A quick phone call beats a stop-work order by a lot.

Don’t skip the permit Building without a permit in Plantation creates a permanent record problem. When you sell, the buyer’s inspector will find it. You’ll either have to retroactively permit it (which often means tearing part of it out for inspection access) or disclose it as an unpermitted structure. Either option is worse than pulling the permit upfront.

Attached vs. Freestanding — The Rules Are Different

This is the first thing to nail down, because it changes your submittal requirements significantly.

Type Engineer Drawings Footing Required Miami-Dade NOA
Attached to house Always required — signed & sealed Depends on design Required for all components
Freestanding (>120 sq ft) Typically required Concrete footing required Required for all components
Freestanding (<120 sq ft) May not be required — confirm with city Depends on design Still applies in Broward

An attached pergola is treated as an extension of the building it connects to. That means the structural loads from the pergola transfer to your house framing, and the City needs to see how that connection is engineered. A Florida-licensed structural engineer has to stamp those drawings. You can’t submit contractor sketches — it has to be sealed.

A freestanding pergola avoids the house framing question, but it introduces another: the footings. Florida code requires freestanding accessory structures to have concrete footings that anchor them to the ground. The size and depth of those footings need to be specified — again, by an engineer — because Broward County is a High-Velocity Hurricane Zone.

The Hurricane Zone Factor — This Is the Part Nobody Explains Upfront

Broward County, including every part of Plantation, is classified as a High-Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ). This is the same designation as Miami-Dade. What it means practically: every structural component you use in your pergola — the posts, the beams, the hardware, the anchors — needs to carry a Miami-Dade Notice of Acceptance (NOA) or a Florida Product Approval.

An NOA is a certification that a product has been tested and approved to withstand the wind loads found in the HVHZ. If you’re buying lumber and standard deck hardware at Lowe’s, most of it won’t have an NOA. The pergola kit you saw on Amazon almost certainly doesn’t have one.

This matters because when a Plantation building inspector comes out for your final inspection, they’re going to ask about product approvals. If your materials aren’t approved for the HVHZ, you don’t pass. This is why many Plantation homeowners end up using contractors who specialize in aluminum pergola systems — aluminum products designed for South Florida typically do carry the right certifications.

Dad’s tip Before you buy anything, ask the supplier for the Florida Product Approval number or the Miami-Dade NOA number. Search it on the Florida Building Code Product Approval website (floridabuilding.org) to confirm it’s current. If a supplier can’t give you this, the product probably isn’t HVHZ-certified.

What You’ll Need to Submit

Here’s what a typical pergola permit submittal looks like in Plantation. Your specific project may require additional documents — call Building Safety to confirm before you spend money on drawings.

For most pergola permits you’ll need: a completed Broward County Uniform Permit Application, a site plan or survey showing the pergola’s location and dimensions relative to your property lines, signed and sealed structural drawings (for attached and larger freestanding structures), product approval documentation (NOA or Florida state approval) for all structural components, and a Notice of Commencement if your project value exceeds $2,500.

The permit application fee is $20, plus $10 per page of plans (first page is free). Beyond that, fees are assessed based on the project’s construction value per City Ordinance #2059. Broward County surcharges, building code surcharge fees, and administrative overhead surcharges are all added on top.

Who Can Submit

A licensed contractor registered with the City of Plantation must submit the permit application. If you’re a homeowner doing the work yourself, there’s an Owner/Builder Affidavit process — but understand what you’re taking on. As an owner/builder you become legally responsible for code compliance, hiring properly licensed subcontractors, and passing inspections. For a simple freestanding structure it’s manageable; for a complex attached pergola, I’d just hire a contractor.

Setbacks — Where Your Pergola Can Actually Go

Before you finalize a location, check your zoning setback requirements. Plantation zoning ordinances specify minimum distances your structure must maintain from property lines, the rear lot line, easements, and other structures. These vary by zoning designation.

The Planning, Zoning & Economic Development department handles zoning questions. If you’re not sure of your setbacks, call them or look up your parcel on the City’s GIS map. Getting the location wrong means either relocating the structure or applying for a variance — neither is something you want to deal with mid-project.

Dad’s tip Pull your property survey before you plan anything. It shows your lot lines, easements, and any recorded restrictions. If you don’t have one, the county property appraiser’s website may have a plat map that gets you close enough to start the conversation with the city.

Your HOA and the City Are Two Different Problems

If you live in a neighborhood with a homeowners association, you need HOA approval in addition to the city permit. These are completely separate processes, and one doesn’t satisfy the other.

As of May 8, 2023, the City of Plantation officially removed HOA approval as a condition for issuing a building permit. In other words, the City will issue your permit regardless of what your HOA says. But the fine print matters: the City’s permit doesn’t override your HOA’s covenants. If your HOA later finds out you built something without their approval and it violates your deed restrictions, they can still take action against you. The City isn’t going to step in on your behalf.

Get HOA approval before you break ground. The city permit says you’re legal with the government. The HOA approval says you’re legal with your neighborhood. You need both.

Most HOAs have an Architectural Review Committee (ARC) that handles these requests. Submit your plans — including dimensions, materials, and a site drawing — and wait for written approval before starting anything. HOA review cycles typically run 30–60 days, so factor that into your timeline.

How to Apply and What to Expect

Plantation offers online permit submission through the E-Permit portal at aca.plantation.org. For more complex projects — like a structurally engineered attached pergola — it may be worth calling Building Safety first to confirm your submittal package is complete before you upload everything.

Once submitted, plan review typically takes one to two weeks, though that can stretch during busy seasons. Spring and summer are peak in South Florida. If the reviewer sends comments — which is common on first submission — revising and resubmitting adds more time. Build that buffer into your project plan.

Your permit is valid for 180 days from the issue date, or 90 days after an approved inspection. If your project gets delayed, you can apply for an extension before it lapses — the form is on the Building Safety page.

The Realistic Timeline

Here’s an honest picture of what getting a pergola permitted in Plantation actually looks like from decision to build. Start with HOA approval — submit your plans to the ARC and expect 30–60 days. While that’s pending, get your engineer drawings done (1–3 weeks depending on the engineer’s availability and project complexity). Once you have both, assemble your full permit submittal and upload it. City review takes 1–2 weeks on first submission. Comments and resubmission add another week or more. Once approved, you pick up the permit and can legally break ground.

Realistically, from “I want a pergola” to “I can legally start building one,” you’re looking at 2–4 months if everything goes reasonably smoothly. Plan for it.

Don’t start building at “permit pending” Starting construction before the permit is issued — even with the application already submitted — is a code violation. A neighbor complaint or a code enforcement drive-by can result in a stop-work order and fines. Wait for the permit in hand before the first post goes in the ground.

The Short Version

Getting a pergola permit in Plantation is doable, but it’s not trivial. The key questions to answer: Is your structure attached or freestanding? Do your materials have Miami-Dade NOA or Florida Product Approval? Do you need a sealed engineer drawing? Has your HOA reviewed and approved the plans? Have you confirmed your setbacks?

Call Building Safety at (954) 797-2765 before you commit to anything. That’s a free conversation that can save you weeks of rework and hundreds in wasted materials. Take it.
