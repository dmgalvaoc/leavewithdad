The Pergola Permit Nightmare in Plantation, FL — And How to Avoid It

I wanted shade. What I got was a crash course in municipal building permits, Florida Building Code, sealed engineering drawings, and why you should never assume a backyard project is "no big deal" in South Florida.

D
Dad
June 2026 · 7 min read · Plantation, FL

Here's what I thought a pergola permit in Plantation would look like: fill out a form, pay a small fee, wait a week, get approved. Here's what it actually looked like: multiple trips to two different city departments, a structural engineer, a digital plan submission through a portal that only accepts licensed contractor accounts, fees I wasn't expecting, and a timeline that stretched from "I want shade by summer" to "maybe by fall." This is that story — and everything you need to know before you start yours.

First Question: Does Your Pergola Actually Need a Permit?

In Florida, the answer is almost always yes — and in Plantation specifically, don't assume otherwise. The City of Plantation requires a building permit prior to the construction, enlargement, or alteration of any structure. A pergola qualifies.

The two main variables are whether it's attached or freestanding, and what size it is.

Type Size Threshold Permit Required? Engineer Plans?
Attached (to home) Any size Yes Almost always
Freestanding Over ~120 sq ft Yes Likely yes in FL
Freestanding Under ~120 sq ft Verify with city Possibly not

If your pergola is attached to the house — even partially — it triggers the Florida Building Code's structural provisions. That means the attachment point needs to be engineered for wind loads. In South Florida, those wind load requirements are not a formality. We're in hurricane territory, and the City takes that seriously.

Don't skip the permit Unpermitted structures in Plantation can surface during home inspections, trigger fines, and — worst case — require demolition at your cost. If you ever sell the house, an unpermitted pergola attached to the structure is a problem you'll own. Do it right.

Who Actually Submits the Permit Application

This is where I hit my first wall. As of November 14, 2022, the City of Plantation only accepts permit applications digitally through the Accela Citizen Access (ACA) portal. No paper, no walk-in submissions for new applications.

And here's the part that caught me: only licensed contractors can submit applications through the portal. Homeowners can log into the citizen portal to check status or pay fees, but initiating a new permit application requires a licensed contractor account registered with the City of Plantation Department of Building Safety.

That means if you're planning to build this yourself, you either need to pull the permit through a licensed contractor (who assumes responsibility for the work), or you need to look into whether Plantation allows owner-builder permits for this type of structure — and call the Building Department directly to confirm before assuming anything.

Dad's tip Before you commit to a contractor, confirm they are registered in Plantation's Accela system. Not every licensed contractor is set up in every city's portal, and some smaller outfits won't be. Ask specifically: "Are you registered to pull permits with the City of Plantation?" If they hesitate, that's your answer.

What the City Requires: The Submittal Checklist

The Department of Building Safety is at 401 NW 70th Terrace (not City Hall — different address). Their phone is (954) 797-2765. For a pergola or patio cover, you'll generally need the following submitted digitally through the ACA portal:

  • Completed building permit application
  • Site plan showing the pergola location relative to property lines and the home
  • Construction drawings — signed and sealed by a Florida-licensed engineer if structural
  • Product approval documentation (if using a prefab or aluminum pergola kit) or engineer-sealed custom drawings
  • Broward County Product Approval submittal form (if applicable), signed and sealed by the Engineer of Record
  • Soil Engineer's statement if the structure requires new footings

For an attached pergola, expect to need sealed engineering drawings almost without exception. For a freestanding structure using a pre-engineered aluminum system (like a louvered pergola with Florida Product Approval), the process can be simpler — the manufacturer's approval documents may substitute for custom engineering, depending on what's in the approval package.

The Fees — And There Are Several

Permit fees in Plantation are layered. There's no single number I can give you without knowing your project, but here's how they stack:

Fee Type Amount
Plan review — first page $20
Plan review — each additional page $10
Construction permit fee Based on project value/sq ft per City Ordinance #2059
Broward County surcharge Added to all permits
Building Code surcharge Added to all permits
Administrative/Overhead surcharge Added to all permits

For a residential pergola, the total permit fees typically run a few hundred dollars depending on the size and value of the project. Your contractor will usually build this into their quote. If they don't mention permit costs separately, ask — because the permit is your responsibility to track, even if your contractor is pulling it.

Timeline: What "2 to 6 Weeks" Actually Means

The City's plan review timeline for residential structures is generally quoted at two to six weeks from a complete submittal. The emphasis is on complete. If your application is missing anything — an incomplete site plan, an unsigned drawing, a missing product approval document — it comes back to you and the clock restarts.

My experience: one round trip for a missing detail on the site plan added three weeks to the process. My contractor was good but didn't catch that the setback dimensions weren't labeled the way the reviewer expected. One correction, one resubmission, one more wait in the review queue.

The permit process doesn't punish mistakes — it just doesn't rush them either. Plan accordingly.

Realistically, budget four to eight weeks from permit submittal to permit-in-hand before any construction begins. If you're trying to have shade for a specific event or season, work backward from that date and add a healthy buffer.

HOA Is a Separate Fight

The city permit and your HOA approval are completely separate processes. One does not substitute for the other. Most Plantation neighborhoods with an HOA require Architectural Review Committee (ARC) approval before any exterior structure is built — including pergolas, shade structures, and patio covers. That process has its own timeline, its own submittal requirements, and its own reasons to say no.

Start your HOA process before you start your permit process. HOA reviews can take four to six weeks on their own, and getting HOA approval after you've already paid for engineering drawings is demoralizing when they say they want something different.

Dad's tip Ask your HOA for any prior-approved pergola examples in the neighborhood. If your design closely matches something that's already been approved, your ARC review will go much faster. Committees approve what they've already approved.

Setbacks: How Close to the Property Line Can You Go?

Your site plan needs to show setback compliance — the minimum distance from your structure to each property line. In most Plantation residential zones, accessory structures must maintain a rear setback of around 7.5 feet and a side setback of 5 feet, but this varies by zoning district. Look up your specific zoning on the City's GIS portal or call the Building Department and give them your address — they'll tell you exactly what applies.

Lot coverage is also a factor. Broward County and Plantation both have limits on what percentage of your lot can be covered by impervious or roofed surfaces. If you've already got a covered lanai, a large pool deck, and a shed, adding a pergola with a solid roof might push you over the lot coverage limit. An open-lattice pergola (technically not a "roof") is treated differently than a solid or louvered roof structure — and that distinction matters for permitting too.

The Short Version — What I'd Do Differently

If I were starting this over, here's the sequence I'd follow:

1. Call the Building Department first. Give them your address, describe what you're planning (attached vs. freestanding, size, material), and ask what's specifically required for your project. Ten minutes on the phone can save you weeks of guesswork.

2. Get HOA approval before spending money on engineering. HOA requirements sometimes dictate the design in ways that change the structural drawings entirely. Approve the concept first.

3. Hire a contractor already registered with the City. Confirm this before signing anything. An unlicensed or unregistered contractor creates problems for you, not just for them.

4. Budget for the permit in your total project cost. For a pergola in the $5,000–$15,000 range, permit fees will typically add $300–$600 depending on project value and the number of plan sheets. Engineering drawings, if needed, are separate and run $400–$1,000+ depending on the engineer.

5. Build in extra time. Eight weeks from permit submittal to inspection-passed is a realistic planning horizon, not a worst case.

It took me longer than it should have. Now it doesn't have to take you as long.
